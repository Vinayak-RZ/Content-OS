## The Person

Vinayak Raizada. Sophomore, B.Tech Electrical Engineering, IIT Roorkee (batch of '28).
Co-founder of Stamped. Builder. Extrovert. Genuinely fun guy.

He is someone who, when he starts explaining something, goes deep. Not because he's performing — because he actually finds it interesting. He makes a lot of jokes. He enjoys life. He takes his work seriously without taking himself too seriously. His private Instagram bio reads *"mother"* — short for motherfucker at heart. That is not contradiction. That is exactly the balance.

He's the kind of person who is loud in a room and thoughtful in a document.

## Early Life — Before the IIT Story

Born and raised across a few cities due to his father's government job transfers.

Early schooling was decent. Not exceptional. He was fine at academics, not particularly sporty, not particularly anything yet. The kind of kid who was doing okay — present, curious, but not yet lit up by anything specific.

The move to Allahabad was significant. He joined **St. Joseph's College (SJC)** — a boys-only school — and attended regularly through class 8. Then COVID hit.

---

## Class 9–10 — NTSE and the First Real Click

When COVID hit in class 9, everything went online. Classes became background noise.

More importantly, class 9 is when NTSE preparation happened. And that was genuinely important.

NTSE — specifically the mental ability sections, the maths, the physics — was the first thing that actually *clicked*. Not because someone told him to care, but because he was good at it and it felt real. That's when the direction toward PCM, toward engineering, toward EE, became less of a default and more of an actual leaning.

He scored 97% in class 10. Decent at computer science (Java, class 10 level). Not great at English and Hindi scoring — but that's marks, not ability. His English is sharp. It's just that exam-English and real English are different things.

---

## JEE — The Honest Version

After class 10, he moved back to Lucknow and joined **Fizicks** (Fiitjee) for JEE preparation. Evening batch initially, performed well enough to shift to the morning batch — which was the better cohort.

For the first one and a half years, the preparation was solid. He was doing well. Then around November, when the syllabus finished, something shifted. The monotony caught up. The motivation drained. Life became flat.

The last six months — covering boards, mock seasons, and right through JEE Advanced — were, in his own words, "quite rusty. Genuinely quite rusty."

He owns this. It's not something he explains away. He lost the plot for a stretch, and he knows it.

He still scored around **rank 5000 in JEE Advanced**. Got **P&I (Production & Industrial Engineering) at IIT Roorkee**. Not the branch he wanted. Not the rank he was capable of if the last six months had gone differently. But he got in.

That gap between potential and outcome — he's aware of it. It shows up later in how he operates: intensity in bursts, then course-correcting when he notices drift.

---

## Arriving at IIT Roorkee

Before arriving, his sister — an engineer herself — told him to start DSA in C++. That's how you get hired. So he started. Came in with some coding context, some exposure to competitive programming culture, some sense that this was important.

Two things were immediately clear on arrival:

1. Competitive programming was *the* culture for getting placed from IITs.
2. P&I was not the branch he wanted to be in.

He targeted both problems simultaneously.

For branching: talked to seniors extensively. The advice was to maintain a strong CG, aim for circuit branches. He scored a **9.5 SGPA in semester 1** — strong enough to get his branch changed from P&I to **Electrical Engineering**. He did it. Branch changed. Problem solved.

For his development path: he was drawn to PAG (the competitive programming club) early on, but what actually grabbed him more deeply was **MDG Space** — the tech club with a startup-oriented ecosystem and the kind of projects that felt real.

---

## MDG Space — Where Things Actually Started

He applied for a mentorship program at MDG Space in season of code (December 2023). The project: build a 2D car racing game (Hill Climb Racing-style) with hand-written physics in Unity/C#. No engine physics. Custom from scratch.

He got selected. He built it. And something opened up.

Game development became the first technical domain that felt like *his*. Not assigned. Not strategic. Actually interesting.

After the mentorship, recruitment tests happened across every major dev club in IITR. He tried for all three development clubs. He got into **MDG Space** — the one that mattered most to him.

---

## Semester 2 — Learning at Volume

Inside MDG, the learning was extensive and fast. Lectures across everything: HTML, CSS, JS, React, Node, Express, Django, RAG, ML, Quantum Computing, Hardware, System Design. Both from seniors (sophmore-level basics) and from 4th years passing down knowledge.

He absorbed it. Made multiple projects — some MDG assignments, some external competitions.

One highlight: **Technical GC Gold at IIT Roorkee** — competing as a team of second-years, winning against older batches. His contribution spanned both React and Unity. A decent achievement by any measure. A significant one given the year.

---

## Summer Before Semester 3 — Going Deeper

Summer break. No coursework. He built two games — one in Unity, one in Godot. Also started getting into Web3: Solidity, how different chains work, how DeFi is structured. Submitted a game for a hackathon. Didn't win, but shipped something hard to finish.

By this point, the direction had shifted from "learning everything" to "building real things."

---

## Semester 3 — The Acceleration

This is where the density really picks up.

**UniteDefine hackathon** — cross-chain transactions, EVM vs non-EVM. Explored Haskell at a surface level. Touched Rust, Solana, other Web3-native languages. Didn't ship a proper product, but the exposure was real.

**ETHGlobal New Delhi** — presented the game built over summer, improved significantly before presenting. Won. $3,000 as a team. **Won the Flow track.** More importantly: 50 hours, 4–5 hours of sleep, running on caffeine, pitching to multiple people. Exhausting. Thrilling. The kind of experience you don't forget.

**Flow Hackathon** (post-ETHGlobal, by the chain they'd won at) — built two things simultaneously: improved the game with multiplayer, and designed **Chrono** — a novel time-based DeFi lending protocol built from scratch. Studied DeFi extensively. Reverse-engineered existing protocols. Built a custom interest rate model with mathematical modeling.

They had a strong shot at winning. They didn't win. The deployed website wasn't opening — a wrong link that hadn't been changed. A silly mistake that cost them the win. He remembers it.

That loss — specifically that loss — sharpened something. Not bitterness. Clarity. He knew the quality of what they'd built. He knew they could've won. The gap between output quality and result was a link that wasn't updated. That kind of thing sticks.

Around this time, the thinking toward a startup was solidifying — not as a single moment but as a gradual accumulation. ETHGlobal proved he could build at a high level under pressure. Chrono proved he could go deep in a new domain fast. The Flow loss proved that execution detail matters at every layer. All of this was pointing somewhere.

---

## The GSOC Moment — One More Data Point

While working on image authenticity research, he also wanted to contribute to open source — specifically LLVM (compiler infrastructure). Raised a PR around Fortran compiler issues.

Then something became obvious: he was adding a small piece to a large thing that wasn't his. And he could instead be spending that exact time on something that *was* his — something real, something with a shot at actual users.

He stopped working on GSOC. Redirected everything to Stamped.

It wasn't a dramatic pivot or a founder epiphany. It was just another quiet data point in a long accumulation — the same accumulation that included ETHGlobal, the Chrono loss, the image watermarking research, the conversations with Utso. The founder identity didn't arrive in a single moment. It built up across semesters, across projects, across small recognitions that kept pointing in the same direction.

---

## The Merge Conflict Hackathon (Semester 4 Start)

At the start of semester 4, he and Utso co-led the organization of **Merge Conflict** — a hackathon run by MDG with a prize pool of over ₹1.5 lakh in cash plus significant credits and partner rewards (~$25,000 total). Reaching out to companies, coordinating logistics, managing everything end-to-end.

It went well. Seeing something he built (the event itself, not just a product) come together at that scale — that was a different kind of feeling.

---

## The Decision — Going All In

Around January 17th of semester 4, he and Utso made the call: all in on the startup.

The alternative was intern season — CP, DSA prep, the standard IIT placement track. He looked at it clearly and decided it wasn't where he wanted to put the next year.

That was not a casual decision. Intern season at IITs is the primary track most students optimize for from day one. Opting out of it is opting out of a safety net.

He opted out.
