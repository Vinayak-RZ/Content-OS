# platform-context.md - Platform-Specific Writing Guide

> This file tells the agentic system how to adapt Vinayak's voice
> for each platform. The voice stays the same. The format, length,
> tone calibration, and structure change.
> Always read writing-style.md first. This file layers on top of it.

---

## LinkedIn

**Audience:** Founders, developers, investors, recruiters, peers in the IIT/startup ecosystem.

**What works:**
- Opens with a single punchy line that stands alone - no context, no intro
- Short paragraphs, one to three sentences each
- Generous whitespace - every paragraph is separated
- A real insight or observation, not just a story summary
- Ends with a thought, not a call to action
- Can reference Stamped, MDG, hackathons, IIT Roorkee - these are credibility anchors here
- Technical depth is welcome but not required - the audience is mixed

**What doesn't work:**
- Long walls of text
- Overly corporate opener ("Excited to share...")
- Generic inspiration
- Hashtag spam at the end
- Asking for engagement explicitly

**Length:** 100–250 words is the sweet spot. Can go up to 350 if the story genuinely earns it.

**Tone:** Polished but not stiff. Honest. Occasionally direct to the point of being blunt. A moment of humor is fine and actually makes it stand out.

**Post structure that works for him:**
```
[Single hook line - no explanation]

[2-3 short paragraphs of actual content]

[Closing line that lands - not a question, not a CTA]
```

**Tone:** Polished but not stiff. Honest. Occasionally direct to the point of being blunt. A moment of humor is fine and makes it stand out.

## Instagram

**Audience:** Peers, college network, general young audience, people who follow founders/builders casually.

**What works:**
- More personal, more casual than LinkedIn
- Shorter - caption should be tight
- Can lead with a moment or image context, then expand
- Humor and personality show up more here
- Story-first, insight second (reverse of LinkedIn)
- Emojis slightly more acceptable here but still not overdone

**What doesn't work:**
- Long analytical takes - save those for LinkedIn
- Heavy technical content without a personal hook
- Trying to sound like a thought leader

**Length:** 50–120 words. Short is better. The image/visual carries weight here.

**Tone:** Casual, real, slightly irreverent. Closest to how he actually talks.

**Post structure that works:**
```
[Scene or moment - one or two lines]

[What it meant or what he noticed]

[One closing line - punchy or slightly funny]
```

**Tone:** Casual, real, slightly irreverent. Closest to how he actually talks.

## X / Twitter

**Audience:** Tech Twitter - developers, founders, AI people, startup ecosystem.

**What works:**
- Hot takes and direct opinions
- Technical observations stated plainly
- Threads when there's actually enough to say
- Reacting to something specific that just happened
- Zig vs Rust type debates - this audience eats that up
- Short and confident - no hedging

**What doesn't work:**
- Long-form narrative (save for LinkedIn or blog)
- Vague motivational posts
- Anything that requires context to land

**Length:** Single tweet: under 200 characters ideally. Thread: 4–7 tweets max, each self-contained.

**Tone:** Most direct of all platforms. Closest to pure opinion. Can be slightly provocative.

**Post structure for single tweet:**
```
[Direct statement or observation - no setup needed]
```

**Post structure for thread:**
```
Tweet 1: [Hook - the main point or strongest claim]
Tweets 2-5: [Each expands one idea, self-contained]
Last tweet: [Sharpest line - lands the whole thread]
```

## Blog / Long-Form

**Audience:** Anyone who finds it - search, shares, personal site readers.

**What works:**
- Full story with real details and specific moments
- Technical depth that goes further than a post can
- Personal voice throughout - not article-writing voice
- Humor and asides are welcome
- Specific numbers, names, decisions - not abstracted
- Reads like a person wrote it, not an article template

**What doesn't work:**
- Generic intro that restates the title
- Structured like a listicle without being one
- Conclusion that just summarizes what was already said

**Length:** 600–1500 words. Should feel complete, not padded.

**Tone:** Most personal register. Closest to actually talking. Can meander slightly if the story earns it.

## Cross-Platform Adaptation Rule

When adapting the same idea across platforms:
- LinkedIn: lead with the insight, back it with the story
- Instagram: lead with the moment, land with the insight
- Twitter: just state the opinion - no story, no setup
- Blog: tell the full story, let the insight emerge from it

The idea doesn't change. The entry point and depth do.

## What Makes It Look Human-Written

This is the most important section. A post can follow every structural rule above and still feel AI-generated. Here is what separates Vinayak's writing from something that was clearly produced by a machine:

**Imperfect specificity.** Humans remember weird details. The time was 3pm, not "late afternoon." The team had 2 developers and 2 designers, not "a small team." One of the developers didn't know Unity. These details are not there to be informative - they're there because a real person noticed them. AI tends to abstract. He doesn't.

**One thing that doesn't quite fit the narrative.** Real writing has a moment where the writer admits something slightly contradictory, inconvenient, or funny about the situation. "The problem statement was a 3-level platformer - not the most interesting, honestly a bit of a let-down." That kind of honesty breaks the AI pattern immediately.

**Incomplete sentences used intentionally.** Not from poor grammar. For rhythm. "Built it in a weekend. It worked. We shipped it." That's a choice, not a mistake.

**An opinion that isn't universally safe.** AI defaults to balanced takes. He doesn't. If he thinks Django is overused, he says it. If something disappointed him, he says it. The presence of an opinion someone could disagree with is a signal that a person wrote this.

**One moment that's slightly too personal or too casual for the platform.** A small joke. A small admission. Something that a brand would never post but a person would. Not a lot - one moment. It does the work.

**No summary sentence.** AI loves to open with "In this post, I'll be sharing..." or close with "These are the lessons I learned from X." Real writing just does the thing. It doesn't announce itself.

**Sentences that start with And or But.** Grammatically frowned upon. Conversationally natural. Use occasionally. It breaks the formal pattern and reads like someone thinking, not drafting.

**Uneven paragraph length.** AI tends to produce paragraphs of similar length. Real writing doesn't. One paragraph is two sentences. The next is six words. The next is a full thought. The variation itself is a signal of humanity.

**The ending doesn't wrap everything up cleanly.** Real endings often leave something slightly open, or land on a feeling rather than a conclusion. AI endings tend to resolve neatly. His don't have to.

## Final Check Before Publishing

Read the draft out loud - or imagine reading it out loud. If any line makes you pause because it sounds like something a robot would say, rewrite that line. The standard is: could Vinayak have said this in conversation? If yes, it stays. If it only sounds right in a document, it probably needs to go.
