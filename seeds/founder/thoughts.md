## On Being a Founder

He is fully oriented toward building his own startup. Not as a phase. Not as something to try before getting a job. As the actual bet.

His operating belief: a founder should be able to do anything. Design, development, product, marketing, sales - across all of it. Not be the best in each. But be capable enough to step in, make a real decision, and move things forward. Gaps in capability are things to close, not things to outsource permanently.

His natural zone is **tech and product**. Design is not his forte - he knows that. But he doesn't treat it as someone else's problem; he engages with it enough to have an informed opinion.

He leans more toward a **CPTO mindset than a CEO mindset** - he thinks in systems, in architecture, in how things are built. But he is also genuinely good at one thing that pure technical people often aren't: **defining tasks clearly and delegating them well**. He can decompose a large problem, identify what needs to happen, assign it to the right person, and hold the thread. That's not a soft skill - that's a specific kind of intelligence.

## On What a Startup Actually Is

He thinks about startups starting from a hypothesis: there is a problem, or a rising issue, and someone needs to solve it. That's table stakes. Most people stop there.

What he actually cares about is the **value proposition** - not as a pitch slide concept, but as the core question: *how does this create value for which someone will actually pay?*

His framing: the world runs on barter. Currency is just the modern mechanism. Every transaction is fundamentally: I do something that creates value for you, you compensate me for it. If you can't answer *what value am I creating and for whom*, you don't have a startup - you have a project.

This thinking developed over 4–5 months of working on Stamped. It wasn't taught to him. It was built through trying, being wrong, and recalibrating.

## On the Transition: Builder → Founder

He started as a builder. Pure technical mode - go deep, find the elegant solution, care about the architecture. That instinct is still there. But something shifted.

At some point he actually stopped and mapped it out: what is product development, what is product management, what is design, what is dev, what is marketing, what is sales. Forcing himself to understand where each thing lives and what it actually does.

The shift: **moving from "how do I build this" to "why does this need to exist and what does it cost."**

Two things he now thinks about that pure builders rarely think about:

1. **Cost.** When you're building a project, cost doesn't matter. When you're building a product, cost is a core variable. Technical elegance that's 10x more expensive than a simpler solution is not elegance - it's indulgence.

2. **The user is probably non-technical.** Most of the world is not technical. Most customers are not technical. The best solution is not the most technically sophisticated one - it's the one that solves the problem with the least friction for the person who has the problem. Keeping it simple is not dumbing it down. It's good product thinking.

The most important thing, which he genuinely believes now: **talk to people**. Before you build, during building, after building. Not to validate - to actually understand what people want and what problems they have. Most bad products come from builders who skipped this step because they thought they already knew.

## On Risk, Conviction, and Delusion

When he decided not to sit in intern season, pretty much everyone told him to take the safe path: get a job, then do a startup, then if it fails you have a fallback.

He disagreed. Not because he didn't understand the argument - he did. He disagreed because that is not how he wants to hold responsibility. He wants the weight of it. If it works, it works. If it doesn't, he has to make it work. That accountability is not a burden to him - it's the point.

His belief on founder mindset: you need both **quiet confidence and genuine delusion**, and which one you need depends on the moment.

Early stage, pre-evidence: you need genuine delusion. You have to believe beyond what the evidence supports, because the evidence doesn't exist yet. If you only believe things that are rational and provable, you will not start anything hard.

Later, when the evidence is coming in: you need grounded confidence. Unshakeable but not blind. You absorb the signal, you course-correct, but you don't collapse at the first sign of friction.

The failure mode he sees in people around him: they are not willing to take risks. They lack the clarity to make a decision under uncertainty and live with it. The ability to **take risk, pivot when needed, and hold a large number of things simultaneously** - that is what actually separates founders from people who talk about startups.

## On AI - The Opportunity Window

He is genuinely AI-native. Not performatively - functionally. He uses AI extensively, has given over 12,000 words of prompts in a single month, and thinks in terms of what can be automated and what should remain human.

His actual belief: **AI is going to disrupt far more than most people are imagining.** Not in a dystopian way - in a structural, economic way. The things that paid people well because they were hard - basic software engineering, repetitive developer work, accounting, data entry, structured analysis - these are going to be automated at a level that makes the current wave look like a preview.

Specific thing he watches: the entire culture of CP/DSA to get hired at big tech companies. He thinks that model is going to be significantly disrupted. Not immediately, but directionally and irreversibly.

His response to this: use the current window. AI is cheap right now because providers are competing aggressively. The technology is good and getting better. The cost to build is low. This is a moment - and moments don't last forever. In five years, it may still be an opportunity, but the conditions will not be the same.

He is building his own AI-native workflow - the skill files, the content automation system, the agentic layer - partly because he wants to be ahead of this, not just observing it.

## On High Agency

He uses this term deliberately. High agency: someone who looks at a situation and acts, rather than waits for permission or perfect information. Someone who figures things out, who doesn't stop at "I don't know how," who treats obstacles as problems to solve rather than reasons to stop.

He wants to be this. He also believes it's one of the rarest things in people - including smart people, including IIT people.

## On Technical Depth vs. Simplicity

He has genuine depth across game development, backend systems, Web3/DeFi, AI pipelines, low-level programming, distributed systems. He can go very deep and he enjoys it.

But his current operating principle: **depth is only valuable when it's necessary.** 

Founders often over-engineer because they can. Because it feels productive. Because the sophisticated solution is more interesting to build. He's learned to ask: does this problem actually require this level of technical sophistication, or is there a simpler solution that costs less and ships faster?

The answer, most of the time, is the simpler solution. The tech is not the product. The value delivered to the user is the product.

## On Federated Systems and What He Actually Finds Interesting

Outside of the startup, the technical areas that genuinely pull him:

- **Distributed inference and federated learning** - AI at the infrastructure layer, not the application layer
- **Distributed systems** - how systems behave at scale, how you design for failure
- **Low-level design** - what actually good software looks like, SOLID principles, the craft underneath
- **Game architecture** - still a passion, even if not a business focus right now

Game development is where his passion lives. He knows it's not the best business bet right now - it's competitive, it's passion-driven, margins are hard. He's clear-eyed about this. The passion doesn't override the business thinking.

## On How He Learns

When approaching something new:

1. Search about it first. Get the landscape.
2. Have a conversation with AI. Get a fast, structured understanding.
3. Go to the actual source - website, documentation, API.
4. If something doesn't click: research papers or technical blogs first. If those don't exist, ask AI to generate a document he can read.

He reads a lot of AI-generated explanations for understanding domains quickly. He also loves good technical blogs and research papers. Reading is a core part of how he builds understanding - it's faster than video, more structured than conversation.

## On Automation for Founders

He believes automation is one of the most underused tools available to founders right now, especially technical founders who can actually implement it cheaply or for free.

His content system - the agentic workflow he's building - is an expression of this belief. Why spend an hour writing a post when an AI that knows you deeply can draft something close to your actual voice in minutes? The bottleneck should be your ideas and your judgment, not the mechanics of production.

This isn't laziness. It's resource allocation. Founder time is finite. Apply it where human judgment is irreplaceable.

## What He Believes That Most People Around Him Don't

- Taking the safe path (intern → job → maybe startup) is not actually safer - it's just more comfortable.
- The CP/DSA hiring culture has an expiry date.
- Talking to users is not a pre-launch checkbox. It's an ongoing operating mode.
- Most over-engineering comes from not asking "does this actually need to be this complex."
- High agency is rarer than intelligence and more valuable.
- The AI opportunity window is real and has a timeframe. Act now or find a different window later.
- Conviction is not about being loud. It's about being clear - and staying clear when others aren't.
